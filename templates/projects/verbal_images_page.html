Overview of the database (VILD)

Welcome to the Verbal Images in Literature Database (VILD). At the time of writing [30/11/23] VILD contains 94 manually pre-annotated verbal images from a variety of authors and genres. Each verbal image occupies a row of the database; each column adds relevant contextual information (the poem, play or novel hosting the image, its genre, author, the critic who noticed it) as well as stylistic information (the size of the image counted in syllables and words, its location within the hosting text, the presence of metaphor and deixis, and more). Stylistic data is either quantitative (discrete, as in the number of syllables; or continuous as in imageability ratings) or qualitative (nominal: as in the critical comments provided or the semantic domains assigned; or nominal and binary as the presence or absence of a characteristic).

VILD is an offshoot of the larger project "Verbal to visual: the image in poetic discourse", financially supported by the Research Council of Lithuania under grant Nr 09.3.3-LMT-K-712-19-0204 and which ran from September 2020 to August 2022.

Parameters (columns)

Text and context

  • Verbal image: an imageable stretch of text
  • Verbal image + co-text: the verbal image and surrounding text
  • Verbal image (orig. if not EN): the image in its original non-English language
  • Author: who crafted the image, e.g. Shakespeare or Whitman
  • Text: the hosting text, e.g. Othello or In a Station of the Metro
  • Genre: poetry, drama, prose (fiction, essay, news)
  • Line(s): line number(s) in verse texts
  • Noted by: the critic who noticed the image (author-date style)
  • Full comment: the critic's commentary, always including the word 'image'

Stylistic information

  • Sense(s) elicited: visual, aural, olfactory, haptic, gustatory
  • Perception flag (PF): words explicitly signalling that an act of perception is occurring
  • Metaphor: conceptual mapping in SMALL CAPS (e.g. FACES ARE PETALS)
  • Deixis: indexical words pointing to a context — you, today, here, this
  • Foregrounding (position): text-initial or text-final images stand out more
  • Foregrounding (devices): parallelism, negation, list of three, rhyme
  • Size: length in syllables, words, characters, and lines
  • IMAG rate of Head: imageability rating of the most vivid word (The Glasgow Norms)
  • Thematic core: semantic domain label from the USAS tagset (e.g. B1, L3, S2)

Nearly all parameters (except Sense(s) elicited, Size and Thematic core) likely act as image boosters, enhancing the perceived vividness and memorability of a verbal image.

What are verbal images?

Verbal images are stretches of text with a higher-than-chance potential to trigger mental images and vivid sensations in readers. They construct sensorially rich fictional worlds whilst attracting critics' interpretive efforts — eliciting immersive presence effects and paving the way to symbolic significance.

How are they made?

Verbal images often coincide with descriptive phrases, narrative clauses, and image-metaphors, but they are argued to be an even more fundamental unit of literary meaning. Their most prominent characteristics are imageable vocabulary and metaphor, plus an average length of 18 syllables or 13 words. Recurrent ancillary characteristics include stylistic strategies (lists, parallelism, deixis, topic shifts, negation) that act as imagery boosters. Typical semantic domains relate to the body, people, animals, plants, and the environment.

How was the database compiled?

Verbal images were manually collected from academic literary criticism. Two conditions had to be met:

  1. The scholar used the word 'image(s)' in commentary — near-synonyms like 'scene', 'figure' or 'picture' were excluded.
  2. The word 'image(s)' explicitly refers to a specific stretch of text that is quoted or unequivocally recoverable.

About one third of the images were contributed by former student Ugnė Spečiūtė. The compiler is not listed as a critic to prevent personal theorising from affecting the data.

Languages

Mostly English; a few examples in French, Italian, and Spanish, all with accompanying English translations.

Who might use it?

  • A writer, poet, artist or content creator looking for inspiration on how canonical authors described specific entities
  • A teacher wanting customised example sets for imagination or interpretation exercises
  • A literary scholar interested in how verbal images have been interpreted
  • A (cognitive) linguist interested in linguistic resources for sensory experience
  • A psychologist or psycholinguist needing controlled stimuli — verbal images as ROIs for eye-tracking studies
  • An AI researcher or developer wanting well-crafted, highly picturable linguistic prompts

Can I contribute?

Yes — the database is open-ended and growing. You can contribute by:

  • Finding new verbal images meeting both conditions above and emailing them to davide.castiglione@flf.vu.lt (include the original paper as PDF)
  • Assisting with a corpus of academic literary criticism articles
  • Flagging inaccuracies or suggesting improvements — annotation for meter and syntax is particularly needed

Students and passionate readers are also welcome. Contact Davide if you want to be involved in empirical studies, surveys, statistical analysis, or applying the model to other languages.
